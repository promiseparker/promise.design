UX Redesign Mobile Speculative
Case Study — 01

Bayse UX-Improvement

I downloaded Bayse, used it for a week, and found 10 real problems hurting users and revenue. This is what I fixed — and exactly why each change matters.

Role Product Designer
Type Speculative Redesign
Platform iOS · Android
Year 2025
Bayse lets Nigerians trade on real-world outcomes — elections, appointments, sports, culture. Like the stock market, but for things people actually argue about. A job posting brought me to the app. What I found made me redesign it.
The Brief

A job opening became
a design audit.

Bayse (formerly GoWagr) posted a product designer role on social media. Instead of sending a generic portfolio, I downloaded the app, used it as a real user would, and documented every friction point I hit. Within a week I had 10 specific problems — each one a barrier between the user and a trade, and between Bayse and revenue.

This is a speculative redesign. Nothing here is decoration. Every screen change is a direct response to a specific problem in the existing app, tied to a real user outcome and a business case. Target users: Nigerians, ages 15–80. Competitors studied: Polymarket, Kalshi.

01
Readability, Search & Visual Consistency

The app was unreadable
in places. Literally.

The old UI had font sizes as small as 9px. Market names, trade counts, and volume figures — the information users need to decide whether to trade — were barely legible. Colour usage was inconsistent across screens, and there was no search button on the homepage. If you came in looking for a specific market, your only option was to scroll.

Before

9px font sizes, inconsistent layout, no search bar visible

After

Minimum 11px across the app. Search + Browse with clear topic categories

User Impact Users can read every market clearly. Anyone searching for "AFCON" or "CBN" finds it in seconds instead of scrolling until they give up.
Revenue Impact Users who can't read what they're trading on won't trade. A search bar converts intent into action — a user who came in with a specific market in mind now has a direct path to it.
02
Smarter Buy Flow — Balance Awareness & Deposit CTA

The Review button
was lying to users.

This was the biggest flow-breaking bug in the app. A user with ₦490 in their account could type ₦1,500, see "Insufficient Funds" on screen, and still tap the active "Review" button — going through the entire review flow only to hit a wall at the end. A completely pointless journey that ends in frustration and a closed app.

The fix is precise: if you don't have enough, the Review button is disabled. You're told exactly how much more you need, and there's a "Deposit" button right there — at the moment you want to spend money — to add funds instantly.

Before

"Insufficient Funds" shown — but the Review button is still active and tappable

After

Review Order greyed out. "Not enough balance" + "+ Deposit ₦10 to continue" CTA right there

User Impact Users are never sent down a path they can't complete. No wasted journeys. If you can't afford it, you know immediately — and you're given the next step, not a dead end.
Revenue Impact This is direct revenue recovery. In the old flow, a user without enough balance almost certainly closes the app. In the new flow, they're one tap from depositing — caught at the exact moment they want to spend. Every recovered deposit is recovered revenue.
03
"To Win" Section — Potential Gains Before Buying

Show them
the money.

The old buy flow showed "Payout if you're right: ₦842" — buried, small, and easy to overlook. The new design replaces it with a prominent green "To win" section that shows ₦21,000 with +100% return in bold before the user confirms anything. The balance remaining after the trade (₦990) is shown too, so there are no surprises.

Before

"Payout if you're right: ₦842" — easy to miss, no visual emphasis

After

Green "To win" card: ₦21,000 · +100% · Balance after shown. Hard to miss, impossible to misread

User Impact Users know exactly what they stand to gain before committing. No guessing, no anxiety. The green colour and the money illustration make the potential feel real and exciting.
Revenue Impact Showing "+100% return" in green triggers excitement and accelerates the decision to trade. Displaying potential gains before purchase is one of the highest-ROI UX changes a trading platform can make — it's the same reason every investment app leads with returns.
04
Plain Language Explanation for Beginners

Not everyone knows what
₦49 per share means.

Bayse's target audience is Nigerians aged 15–80. Most of them have never used a prediction market. The old app gave them prices and percentages with no explanation of what was actually happening. The redesign adds a plain English breakdown directly below the "To win" section — no jargon, no assumptions.

New Design

"You're buying 110 Yes shares of Atiku Abubakar at ₦49 each. If this outcome happens, you get ₦100 per share back — that's ₦11,000 profit." Anyone can understand that.

User Impact A 15-year-old or a 70-year-old can understand exactly what they're agreeing to. The difference between "I don't get this app" and "Oh — I could make ₦11,000 if Atiku wins" is one sentence.
Revenue Impact This expands Bayse's total addressable market. Right now only people who already understand prediction markets feel confident trading. With plain language, Bayse can convert first-timers — which is where most of the untapped growth lives.
05
Buy/Sell Toggle + Market/Limit Toggle

Buy or Sell? Market or Limit?
Make it impossible to get wrong.

The old buy sheet had a Buy/Sell toggle but nothing else. No distinction between market and limit orders. The new design adds a clean second toggle — Market / Limit — on the right side of the header. Buy is blue. Sell is red. The colour coding does the work before the user even reads the label. Switching to Limit reveals a dedicated Limit Price field, keeping the interface uncluttered until it's needed.

Buy · Market — blue chip, Market/Limit toggle on the right

Buy · No — red chip makes the selection unmistakable

Limit order — Limit Price field appears only when needed

User Impact Users never accidentally sell when they meant to buy — the red on Sell acts as a natural warning. The interface is unambiguous at every state.
Revenue Impact Fewer accidental trades means fewer support requests and fewer refund claims. Limit orders being accessible encourages sophisticated trading, which increases platform liquidity and overall trade volume.
06
Sell Flow — Empty State, Share Count & Instant Sell Access

Don't let users
chase their own tail.

The old sell flow had no protection. A user with no shares could input an amount, tap Review, and go through the whole flow for nothing. There was also no way to see how many shares you owned from the market card itself — you had to dig into a separate screen to find out. The redesign fixes all of this.

If you own shares, your position is now visible directly on the market card — "Yes · 8 shares" inline next to the candidate — with a "Sell X Yes shares" button right there. No navigation required. If you have no shares: a clear empty state with a "Switch to Buy" CTA. If you do own shares: you see exactly how many, their current value, and percentage-based sell chips (10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%) that make choosing an amount immediate.

Before

0 shares — "You'll Receive: ₦0" with no explanation. The Review button stays active, letting users start a flow that goes nowhere

After

"No shares to sell" — clear message, Review greyed out, Switch to Buy CTA. Dead end turned into an opportunity

Position visible on the market card — "Sell 10 Yes shares" appears inline. No extra navigation needed

You own 110 Yes shares · Current value ₦18,340 — everything visible before deciding

Selling 25 shares → "To receive ₦15,420 +80%" — same green card logic as the buy flow

User Impact Users always know where they stand. Position is visible without digging, selling takes one tap from the market card, and dead ends are replaced with clear next steps. No confusion, no wasted flow.
Revenue Impact Faster sell access means faster reinvestment. The "Switch to Buy" button on the empty state turns a dead end into a sales moment. Every removed friction point between a user and their next trade is revenue recovered.
07
Review Order Screen — Redesigned to Match the New Direction

Same information.
Redesigned to feel like it belongs.

The information on the old Review screen was already good — Spending, Payout, fees, estimated shares, profit if correct. The problem wasn't what was there, it was how it looked. The old layout was flat and transactional, visually disconnected from the redesigned buy flow it sat at the end of. This was about bringing it into line: giving Payout the visual dominance it deserves, surfacing the zero-fee message as a confidence signal rather than a quiet table row, and making the whole screen feel like it was designed at the same time as everything else.

Before

All the right data — but Spending and Payout share equal visual weight, and the zero-fee advantage sits silently in a table row

After

Payout ₦30,000 (+80%) given visual dominance. "Zero trading fee on Bayse" surfaced as a trust signal — not buried, shown at the moment it matters most

User Impact A review screen that matches the rest of the flow feels intentional. Users don't notice good design — they just feel confident. That confidence is what gets the Confirm tap.
Revenue Impact Design consistency is a trust signal. A screen that looks like it belongs converts better than one that feels like a different app. Lifetime value is built on moments like this — small, cumulative, compounding.
08
Market Chart — Readable at a Glance

Which line am I reading?
The chart should tell you.

The old chart tooltip showed raw percentages — "43.52%", "24.79%", "18.28%" — with no candidate names attached. To know which line you were looking at, you had to glance back up at the legend while holding the tooltip open. On a fast-moving market, that's friction. The new chart labels every line directly in the tooltip by candidate name, so you always know exactly what you're reading. Time filters were also reordered logically (12H → 1D → 1W → 1M → ALL), and key stats — trade count and volume — now sit below the chart as a quick read.

Before

Tooltip shows "43.52%" — but which candidate? Cross-reference the legend to find out. Time filters in an odd order (1Y, 1M, 1W first)

After

"Atiku Abubakar · 81%" directly in the tooltip — no cross-referencing needed. Logical time filters, trade count and volume visible below

User Impact Users can monitor price movement and read the market in one glance. The chart stops being a chart you have to decode and becomes one you can actually use to make decisions.
Revenue Impact A readable chart builds conviction. Users who understand what they're looking at are more likely to trade — and to trade at higher values. Confusion suppresses bets; clarity enables them.
09
Consolidated Market Rules, Timeline & Payout

Scattered rules
are unread rules.

The old detail page spread market rules, timelines, and payout information across multiple sections that required scrolling and tapping "Show full details" to access. Users had to piece together the answer to a simple question: "When do I get paid if I'm right?" The new design consolidates everything into a single bottom sheet — Market Summary, Rules, Verification Source, Timeline, and Payout — with a dropdown to select the specific outcome you're interested in.

Before

Rules section visible but collapsed behind "Show full details" — timeline is on a separate section further down

After

One bottom sheet: Market Summary → Rules → Verification Source (punch.ng) → Timeline → Payout. Outcome selector at the top.

User Impact Every question a user has before placing a trade — what are the rules, who decides, when do I get paid — is answered in one place, in one scroll. No hunting.
Revenue Impact Informed users bet bigger. Doubt creates small trades or no trades. Clear rules, a named verification source (Punch Nigeria), and a concrete payout timeline (4–12 hours) build the confidence needed for higher trade values.
10
Related Markets — "People Are Also Trading"

Keep them
in the market.

After reading a market, the old app had no way to discover related ones without going back to the feed. The redesign adds a "People are also trading" section that surfaces related markets with thumbnails, titles, and chance percentages. If you're reading about the ADC presidential candidate, you're probably also interested in governor defections, party switches, and Tinubu's next move.

Before

Related Markets tab exists but is passive — no visual pull, no trade counts visible at a glance

After

"People are also trading" — inline, visually active, with thumbnails and chance percentages. Each card is a potential next trade.

User Impact Users discover markets without going back to search. The session continues naturally — one market leads to the next, the way a good feed should work.
Revenue Impact This is cross-selling — the same principle Amazon uses with "Customers also bought." Every related market a user clicks is a potential trade. More trades per session equals more revenue per user.
11
Comments & Opinion Sharing — New Feature

Added: a place
to share your take.

The old app had no way to share an opinion. You could trade — but you couldn't say why, read what others thought, or engage with the market beyond placing a bet. This wasn't an improvement to something that existed. It's a feature that didn't exist at all.

The new design adds a full comments section directly on the market detail page. Users discuss reasoning, share information, and challenge each other's takes. Trade size badges next to usernames (e.g. "2.4K Atiku") make the community feel like informed participants, not anonymous noise — your position gives your opinion weight.

Before

No place to share an opinion. The market detail shows the chart, the candidates, the buy buttons — and nothing else. Read, trade, leave.

After

@lagospredict, @betwise_ng, @tradeking99 — users sharing takes, debating outcomes, signalling conviction. Trade badges show how much skin each person has in the game.

User Impact Trading becomes social. Users come back not just to place trades but to see what others are saying, defend their position, and react to new information. The app gets stickier with every comment thread.
Revenue Impact Social features consistently drive 2–3× higher retention on trading platforms. More time in the app means more exposure to markets, which means more trades. DAU goes up. Revenue follows.
The Takeaway

Good UX isn't decoration.
It's a revenue engine.

Every one of these 10 changes ties directly to the same two outcomes: make it easier for users to trade, and make more money for Bayse. The fixes range from a single button state (disabling Review when funds are insufficient) to a full feature addition (Comments). But none of them are cosmetic. Each one removes a specific reason a user might stop, leave, or lose trust — and replaces it with a reason to stay and trade.

Bayse has a real product with real traction. The underlying idea — prediction markets built for Nigerian culture — is strong. What it needs is a UX that matches that ambition. This redesign is my argument for what that looks like.
