Mobile App Interaction Design Smart Home
Case Study — 02

Lumino

Designed and built a smart home lighting control app from scratch — combining tactile interaction design with real engineering to create something that feels as physical as flipping a switch.

Role Designer & Developer
Timeline 12 Weeks
Platform iOS · Web
Year 2024
Smart home apps promised to simplify life. Most delivered a control panel. Lumino was built to feel less like configuration and more like a conversation with your space.
The Challenge

Powerful hardware,
forgettable software.

Smart lighting hardware has come a long way. The apps controlling it haven't. Most smart home apps are built around the assumption that users want granular control — and present them with dashboards, sliders, menus, and settings that feel better suited to a network router than a living room lamp.

The result is that people with perfectly capable smart bulbs end up using a physical switch — not because the app is broken, but because the experience isn't worth the friction. Lumino was a challenge to close that gap: make the software feel as immediate and satisfying as the hardware it controls.

The Problem

Existing smart home apps front-load complexity. Users land on room lists, device trees, or app-home dashboards before they've done anything useful. There's no sense of immediacy — no feeling that the app and the physical world are connected.

Design Goals

  • Make turning a light on feel as fast as a switch
  • Show the light's actual state — not just a toggle
  • Design for glanceability: no hunting for controls
  • Build it — not just mock it — to test real interaction feel
Research & Discovery

People don't think in
settings. They think in moods.

I started with observation — not surveys. I watched how people actually interact with smart lighting in their homes. The pattern was consistent: nobody opened an app to set a Kelvin temperature. They wanted to make a room feel right, quickly. They thought in terms of what they were doing — reading, relaxing, watching something — not what the bulb was emitting.

Interviews reinforced this. Users described their ideal lighting experience in sensory terms: "warmer," "dimmer," "softer." They wanted the app to speak that language. Technical precision mattered to nobody. Immediacy and feedback mattered to everyone.

Light Off

The off state communicates clearly — the cone fades, the bulb dims. No ambiguity about what's on or off.

Light On

When the light is on, the whole screen responds. The beam fills the space, the colour matches the bulb, and the toggle confirms it instantly.

Research Methods

  • In-home observations with 8 smart home users
  • Contextual interviews — "walk me through your evening"
  • Competitive teardown of 6 smart home apps
  • Usability testing across 3 interactive prototype rounds

Key Insights

  • Users abandoned apps within 30 seconds if nothing happened visually
  • Scenes were used more than individual controls — context beats precision
  • Schedules were a high-value, low-use feature: powerful but intimidating to set up
  • The biggest trust signal was visual feedback — seeing the change happen
Designed & Built

Motion that earns
its place.

Lumino was built in React with React Router v7, Tailwind CSS v4, and Motion for animation. The decision to build rather than prototype in Figma was deliberate — spring physics can't be faked in a static file. Real interaction had to be tested with real code.

Every transition uses spring physics via the Motion library. Sliders don't snap — they settle. The beam cone doesn't cut from dim to bright — it breathes. The Web Audio API powers subtle haptic-adjacent audio feedback that reinforces physical actions without being intrusive. The result is an interface that has weight. When you interact with Lumino, you feel like you've touched something real.

Tech Stack

  • React + React Router v7 — component architecture and routing
  • Tailwind CSS v4 — utility-first responsive styling
  • Motion — spring-physics animations throughout
  • React Context — global light state management
  • Web Audio API — subtle interaction sound feedback

Animation Principles

  • Spring physics only — no linear or ease curves
  • The beam responds to brightness in real time
  • Colour transitions blend through the colour space, not hard-cut
  • State changes are never instant — always felt
Core Interaction

Brightness you see,
not just set.

The central design decision in Lumino is the live beam visualisation. Rather than showing a percentage in a text field or a progress bar, the light's output fills the screen as an animated cone — narrower and paler at low brightness, wide and saturated at full power.

The vertical slider on the right edge keeps controls peripheral — present when needed, invisible when not. As you drag, a floating pill callout surfaces the exact percentage, giving precision without cluttering the default view. The beam and the number tell the same story simultaneously.

Low brightness — the beam narrows and softens. The screen itself gets quieter.

Full brightness — the cone expands, colour saturates. The screen becomes the light.

Mid-drag precision — the floating callout surfaces the exact value without cluttering the idle state.

Colour Customisation

Full spectrum.
Zero confusion.

Colour is where smart lighting apps typically either overwhelm or underwhelm. Lumino's colour system gives full RGB control through a continuous spectrum slider, while offering curated presets — Warm, Daylight, Cool, Ocean, Coral, Lavender — for users who think in moods rather than hex codes.

The selected colour renders live in the beam above. There's no "preview" button, no confirmation step before you see the result. The light in the UI is the light in your room — instant, honest feedback. Saving the change is a conscious, deliberate tap — separate from the act of exploring.

The spectrum slider and preset chips live below the live preview. Choose a mode or drag freely — both paths converge on the same result.

Select pink, and the room turns pink. The beam responds instantly — no processing delay, no loading state. What you pick is what you get.

Scheduling

Set it once.
Live with it always.

Schedules were identified in research as a high-value, low-adoption feature. Users wanted automation but gave up during the setup. The issue wasn't capability — it was the interface. Most smart home apps buried scheduling three levels deep and required understanding concepts like "routines" and "triggers" before a simple timer could be set.

Lumino's schedule system is a single list: what time, which action, which days. Adding one takes four taps. The next trigger time is always visible beneath each rule — "Next: Tomorrow, 7:00 AM" — so users know the system is working without having to verify it. Deletion uses a friction-adding confirmation that prevents accidental removals, but doesn't punish deliberate ones.

The schedule list reads like a sentence: Turn on at 7:00 AM on weekdays. Turn off at 11:00 PM every day.

Adding a schedule is one gesture: time, action, repeat. The bottom sheet keeps context — the existing schedules are visible behind it.

Destructive actions get a moment of deliberation. Not a wall — just a beat. The specific schedule being deleted is called out clearly.

Design Decision

Showing the next trigger time beneath each schedule rule turned an abstract concept — "this will run at some point" — into a concrete, trusted contract. Users knew the app was working without needing to open it to check.

Outcome

Schedule creation in usability testing went from the most-abandoned task to the most-completed. Once users saw how few steps it took, they created multiple schedules in a single session — something that almost never happened in competitive apps.

Outcomes

Built to be felt,
not just seen.

Lumino proved that smart home design doesn't have to be a trade-off between power and approachability. The combination of live visual feedback, spring-physics motion, and simplified flows produced a prototype that testers consistently described the same way: "it just feels right."

More than a design case study, Lumino is a working artefact — a coded, interactive product that closes the gap between what was designed and what was experienced. Every interaction decision was validated in code, not assumed from a mockup.
